1.1	Background of the Study
The rise of digital money services (like mobile banking apps) has led to the emergence of large-scale application platforms that integrate multiple functionalities in one place such as payments, ecommerce, messaging etc, within them, known as Super Apps. These super apps are built using microservice-based architectures, where individual services communicate with each other through application programming interfaces (APIs). While this design makes the app flexible and easier to scale, it creates security risks, especially when handling sensitive information like credit card numbers.

In modern super applications, when users provide their payment card information may be processed, logged, stored temporarily, or handled across multiple internal microservices. As a result, this means raw Primary Account Numbers (PANs) can accidentally become accessible to services that do not strictly require access to such sensitive information. When this happens, it creates more opportunities for potential data breaches and puts organizations at risk of violating regulatory and compliance requirements. Studies on super apps has shown that sensitive secrets and user data often leak across internal components because of complicated service interactions, poor separation between systems, and weak enforcement of access restrictions (Baskaran et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023).

To tackle cardholder data threats, credit card vulnerabilities, transaction security dangers, the Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard (PCI DSS) requires organizations to implement tight restrictions concerning how customer payment credentials is stored, processed, and transmitted. A core principle in PCI DSS is clearly defining which systems are allowed to access raw cardholder data as opposed to those that must be restricted. Standard security methods such as tokenization, encryption, and vault-based architectures are commonly used to minimize access to raw PANs (PCI SSC, 2024a; Basis Theory, 2025). However, although these measures lower danger, organizations often lack realistic approaches to measure and verify which internal services still have access to raw card data in complex distributed systems.

Existing PCI compliance tools and tokenization platforms mainly concentrate on securing information in storage or during transmission, but they offer restricted insight into how cardholder data flows across microservices within a super app. This limitation creates challenges for organizations to confidently determine which services still handle raw PANs and to assess the performance of implemented security controls (URM Consulting, 2025; Deepstrike Labs, 2025). For this reason, there is a growing need for systems that can examine card detail visibility at the service level and show the outcomes in a measurable and understandable form, especially in complex service networks.

1.2	Statement of the Problem
Multi-functional platforms are built upon interconnected microservices to offer seamless user experiences, such as transaction handling. Within these systems, users’ payment card details can traverse several internal services during normal operations. Although security mechanisms such as encryption and tokenization are frequently implemented, organizations frequently lack clear insight into which component services can still access raw card numbers and which ones are properly restricted.
Several established systems and standards address cardholder information safety, especially PCI DSS requirements, tokenization platforms, and encryption-based controls. These approaches provide robust conceptual and cryptographic protection for customer financial details. Though, available systems like PCIvault, IBM guardium and Checkmax mainly concentrate on data protection mechanisms rather than service-level exposure analysis, making it difficult to identify where raw PANs remain visible within complex microservice ecosystems (PCI SSC, 2024b; Kurii & Opirskyy, 2024).
As a result, companies running multi-function platforms encounter a significant problem: they cannot easily determine what number of internal services still see real card numbers after security controls are applied, nor can they quantify the reduction achieved by those controls. This lack of clarity weakens regulatory confidence, makes more difficult threat evaluation, and increases the likelihood of accidental information leaks. There is therefore a need for a system that can detect, classify, and evaluate cardholder data exposure across microservices in an understandable and actionable way.
1.5 Scope of the Study
This research focuses on payment card data handling within a multi-function platform structure consisting of component services. It is limited to the analysis of unencrypted card number visibility during transaction processing and does not cover other forms of personally identifiable information. The implementation is demonstrated using test services and practice payment information in accordance with PCI standards.
1.3 Aim and Objectives
The aim of this study is to design and implement a framework for analyzing payment card data exposure within a multi-function platform setting following the PCI DSS standard, aiming to determine which internal microservices can access raw cardholder data and assessing the success of implemented security measures namely tokenization and encryption.
The specific objectives of the study are as follows:
1.	To design a technical framework for examining card credential accessibility in all-in-one applications.
2.	Categorizing individual card entries based on whether they are exposed to internal services
3.	Finding and documenting which service modules are able to reach actual account numbers.
4.	Quantifying the reduction in payment information accessibility achieved through applied security controls.
5.	Provide graphical and numerical to support compliance assessment and decision-making.

1.4 Research Questions
This study seeks to answer the following research questions:
1.	How can payment card data exposure be analyzed within a multi-function platform with multiple microservices?
2.	To what extent can automated analysis of tokenization and encryption mechanisms determine which system components can see raw PAN after PCI required cryptographic controls are applied?
3.	How can individual card exposure be classified throughout a decentralized platform??
4.	How much consistency and reliability does the proposed framework show in assessing PCI scope relative to traditional, manual scoping approaches?
5.	How can the reduction in payment information accessibility be measured and presented in an understandable way?


1.5 Significance of the Study
By providing a practical framework for identifying and measuring cardholder information accessibility, this research aids stronger alignment with PCI DSS requirements and improves vulnerability awareness in distributed systems. The system additionally adds to academic research by unifying theoretical transaction protection mechanisms and real-world service-level exposure analysis.
1.6 Definition of Operational Terms
For the purpose of this research, the following operational terms are defined as follows:
Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard (PCI DSS): is a global set of security requirements for any organization that stores, processes, or transmits credit/debit card data, aiming to prevent fraud and data breaches by requiring secure handling of cardholder information. In this study, PCI DSS establishes the legal and technical standard against which compliance scope and scope reduction are measured.
PCI Scope: refers to the collection of system components, processes, networks, and services that store, process, transmit, or can impact the security of cardholder data. Within this study, PCI scope is considered as a dynamic attribute affected by system architecture and information movement patterns.
Cardholder Data Environment (CDE): a group of systems and network components that directly handle cardholder data or are connected to systems that do. The CDE defines the primary limit for PCI DSS standard verification in this research.
Primary Account Number (PAN): are the unique payment card number allocated to a cardholder. In this study, PAN represents the most confidential data point whose presence or absence determines PCI scope inclusion.
Tokenization: is the process of replacing sensitive data (like credit card numbers) with non-sensitive, unique codes (tokens) for security. In this study tokenization is analyzed as a primary mechanism for limiting access to active card numbers and minimizing PCI scope.
Encryption: the process of transforming information or data into a code, especially to prevent unauthorized access. In this research, encryption is analyzed in relation to how well it works at protecting cardholder data and influencing PCI scope classification.
Scope Reduction: Refers to the measurable decrease in the number of systems and components that belong to PCI DSS scope as a result of implementing cryptographic controls such as tokenization and encryption.
Cryptography-aware Framework: a technical approach that identifies, analyzes, and measures PCI scope according to visible encryption patterns and data flow patterns within a system, rather than solely on static documentation.
Automated Scope Measurement: the use of systematic, software-supported techniques to experimentally identify PCI scope and scope reduction without reliance on manual interviews or static data flow diagrams.
Residual Data Exposure: unwanted continuation of sensitive cardholder data within system components such as logs, backups, caches, or tracking systems, that might lead to systems to remain within PCI scope even with the application of cryptographic controls.
Continuous Compliance: A method for PCI DSS compliance that focuses on continuous monitoring, verification, and measurement of control effectiveness rather than periodic, point-in-time assessments.
